This module provides a sector-specific analysis of the consequences of climate change and its impacts. It compiles and visualizes data on how climate-related factors, such as temperature fluctuations, shifting precipitation patterns and extreme weather events such as drought and floods, impact key sectors like agriculture, water, energy infrastructure and health. By offering detailed insights into the vulnerabilities and opportunities within each sector, the sectoral climate impact will assist policymakers, businesses, and stakeholders in making informed decisions and crafting targeted strategies for climate adaptation and resilience. It serves as an invaluable resource for identifying sector-specific risks, facilitating proactive mitigation measures, and fostering sustainable development in an era of climate uncertainty.

In Zambia, most livelihoods depend on staple crops like cassava and maize. Increasing temperatures and erratic rainfall patterns have altered crop water requirements and increased the spread and incidence of pests & diseases​

Challenges vary between climate zones, with Region 3 facing humidity and waterlogging, while Region 1 is dominated by maize and livestock. Water availability issues could negatively impact production and hydropower-dependent agricultural processing and manufacturing.

Climate change could lead to an uneven distribution of water resources in Zambia, with 35 percent of the population lacking access to clean water and 55 percent lacking sanitation facilities. Droughts and floods in the north have caused contamination and the spread of waterborne illnesses.​

​​Zambia's ecosystems and services are being stressed by climate variability, with 38% of land protected. Drought and reduced water availability force wildlife to enter human settlements, resulting in conflicts. Fire risk increases in grasslands and southern baikiaea woodlands.​

Zambia's overreliance on climate-sensitive energy resources has been exposed due to decreased rainfall. Hydropower generates the majority of the country's electricity, and reduced river flows negatively impact energy production potential. ​​

Forest biomass is an important energy source for the rural population, and flash floods and heavy precipitation damage roads, bridges and culverts.

Zambians already suffer from climate-sensitive diseases such as malaria and diarrhea, which will likely compound due to climate change.

Annually 4 million Zambians are affected to malaria. potential changes in agricultural productivity may lead to malnutrition, food insecurity, poverty, and fragmented water supply and sanitation.
